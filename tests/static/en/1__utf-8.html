In historic speech, Pope Francis focuses on dignity of human life

By Tracy Jan Globe Staff  September 24, 2015

WASHINGTON – Pope Francis, in a historic address to Congress Thursday, used the broad theme of protecting the dignity of human life – the same reason the church opposes abortion — to call upon US lawmakers to stand up for poor people, welcome immigrants, and end the death penalty.

He invoked the Golden Rule – “Do unto others as you would have them do unto you” – to urge America to open its arms to refugees fleeing strife as well as those seeking to cross borders for better economic opportunity.

“In a word, if we want security, let us give security; if we want life, let us give life; if we want opportunities, let us provide opportunities,” Francis said. “The yardstick we use for others will be the yardstick which time will use for us.”

He obliquely critiqued unfettered capitalism and politicians’ roles in making laws that benefit Wall Street and the wealthy at the expense of the average person.

“If politics must truly be at the service of the human person, it follows that it cannot be a slave to the economy and finance,” he said.

But the pope’s message, while containing many elements that appealed to Democrats, was far from liberal as he made veiled references to the church’s staunch opposition to abortion and gay marriage.

He reminded Congress of its responsibility to “protect and defend human life at every stage of its development.” And he upheld the traditional family as an ideal.

“I cannot hide my concern for the family, which is threatened, perhaps as never before, from within and without,” Francis said. “Fundamental relationships are being called into question, as is the very basis of marriage and the family.”

Thursday was the first time in history that a pontiff has spoken directly to a joint session of the members of United States Senate and House of Representatives, a third of whom are Catholic. He entered the House chamber to rousing applause and shook hands with Secretary of State John Kerry as he approached the rostrum.

Tens of thousands of people surrounded the Capitol and watched his speech, which was broadcast on a large screen.

Francis spoke in heavily accented English in an atmosphere that otherwise was akin to a State of the Union speech, complete with Vice President Joe Biden and House Speaker John Boehner, who invited the pope to the Capitol, seated immediately behind him.

Francis read from sheets of paper, instead of a teleprompter, and peered over his reading glasses to make a point, occasionally gently waving a finger. Lawmakers applauded throughout — often, depending on the issue, with partisan emphasis.

His remarks on the family drew an immediate standing ovation from Republicans; Democrats eventually rose as well as a sign of respect. Four of the Supreme Court justices sitting directly before the pope in the first row – Chief Justice John Roberts and Justices Anthony Kennedy, Sonia Sotomayor and Ruth Bader Ginsburg -- remained in their seats. The court had legalized same-sex marriage in June.

Some of the pope’s strongest language was reserved for the subject of immigration. Invoking the “dreams’’ of equality and freedom articulated by Martin Luther King Jr., he called on the lawmakers to open their arms to immigrants seeking economic and social opportunity.

“Is this not what we want for our own children?’’ he said. “We must not be taken aback by their numbers, but rather view them as persons, seeing their faces and listening to their stories, trying to respond as best we can to their situation.’’

In his speech, Francis reflecting upon the world’s political conflicts and criticized “violent conflict, hatred, and brutal atrocities, committed even in the name of God and of religion.”

No one religion is immune to fundamentalism, he warned, and everyone must be “attentive to every type of fundamentalism, whether religious or of any other kind.”

“A delicate balance is required to combat violence perpetrated in the name of a religion, an ideology or an economic system,” he said, “while also safeguarding religious freedom, intellectual freedom and individual freedoms.”

He also called for ending the global arms trade.
